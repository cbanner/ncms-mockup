Visitor Engagement & Targeting

Understanding the supporter engagement hierarchy and how to use it to personalize the website experience for different visitor types.

As a nonprofit, NCMS interacts with supporters across a spectrum of engagement levels. Understanding this hierarchy helps us recognize where each person is in their relationship with us and create experiences that meet them where they are.

Each level represents not just a depth of commitment, but also a different mindset and set of needs. By understanding these levels, we can tailor our website content and calls-to-action to resonate with each visitor type.

Understanding the Shape

The pyramid narrows at the top because fewer people reach the highest engagement levels. Most website visitors are Observers or Followers. Our goal is to serve each level well while creating clear pathways for those who want to engage more deeply.

Leader

Active organizational leadership

Board members, staff, committee chairs. They guide strategy, make decisions, and represent NCMS in the community.

Examples: Board member, Executive Director, committee chair
Owner

Contributing specialized skills and knowledge

They invest not just time and money, but their professional expertise. They feel genuine ownership over NCMS's success.

Examples: Pro-bono consultant, advisory role, major donor with strategic involvement
Contributor

Giving significant time or money

Regular donors, recurring volunteers, event hosts. They've made NCMS part of their ongoing commitments.

Examples: Monthly donor, regular volunteer, event committee member
Endorser

Sharing trust and occasional time

They believe in NCMS enough to recommend us to others, write reviews, share on social media, or attend occasional events.

Examples: Social media sharer, Google reviewer, event attendee, referral source
Follower

Actively paying attention

They've taken a small action to stay connected: following on social media, subscribing to the newsletter, or visiting the site multiple times.

Examples: Email subscriber, social media follower, repeat website visitor
Observer

Aware of NCMS but anonymous

First-time website visitors, people who've heard of us but haven't engaged. They may be evaluating whether NCMS is relevant to them.

Examples: First-time site visitor, someone who heard about NCMS from a friend
2

Website Targeting Strategies

Practical approaches for serving each visitor type through the website experience

Observer

Convert Awareness to Interest

Who They Are

First-time visitors, often arriving from search or referral. They're evaluating: "Is this relevant to me?" and "Can I trust this organization?"

Website Strategies

  • Lead with mission and impact (not just "what" but "why")
  • Display credibility signals: years of service, student count, testimonials
  • Make programs and pricing easy to find
  • Offer low-commitment first step: "Learn More" or "Get Info"
  • Include clear pathways for different visitor types (families, donors, volunteers)
Primary Goal
Build trust and invite them to take one small action
Follower

Deepen Connection

Who They Are

Repeat visitors, newsletter subscribers, social followers. They're interested but haven't committed. They're asking: "What else can I learn?" and "Is now the right time?"

Website Strategies

  • Feature stories and updates that reward return visits
  • Highlight upcoming events and deadlines (urgency)
  • Provide deeper content: program details, faculty bios, student stories
  • Offer event invitations as a next step (recitals, open houses)
  • Make newsletter signup prominent and valuable
Primary Goal
Move them from passive interest to active participation
Endorser

Empower Advocacy

Who They Are

Current or past students/families, occasional event attendees, people who've shared about NCMS. They trust us and want to help, but may not know how.

Website Strategies

  • Make sharing easy: social buttons, referral links, downloadable assets
  • Invite reviews and testimonials (with clear instructions)
  • Celebrate community: showcase student performances, family stories
  • Offer volunteer opportunities for one-time commitments
  • Provide "invite a friend" or "gift a lesson" options
Primary Goal
Make it easy and rewarding to spread the word
Contributor

Recognize and Retain

Who They Are

Donors, regular volunteers, enrolled families. They've made a commitment. They want to see impact and feel appreciated.

Website Strategies

  • Show impact: "Your support helps fund X scholarships"
  • Provide easy access to donor/family portals if applicable
  • Highlight volunteer stories and donor recognition
  • Offer upgrade paths: "Become a monthly donor" or "Join a committee"
  • Share exclusive updates or behind-the-scenes content
Primary Goal
Reinforce their decision and invite deeper involvement

"The organizations that thrive understand that every visitor is on a journey. Our job isn't to push everyone toward the same action, but to meet each person where they are and offer a meaningful next step."

— Nonprofit engagement best practice

Personalization Approaches

From simple to sophisticated: options for tailoring the website experience

Level 1

Content Segmentation by Audience

Create clear navigation paths for different visitor types: "For Families," "For Donors," "For Volunteers." Each section speaks directly to that audience's needs and motivations. No technology required beyond thoughtful information architecture.

Complexity
Low
Level 2

Smart CTAs Based on Page Context

Show different calls-to-action depending on the page. Visitors on program pages see "Enroll Now." Visitors on impact/story pages see "Support Our Mission." Match the ask to the content they're already engaging with.

Complexity
Low
Level 3

New vs. Returning Visitor Detection

Use cookies to detect returning visitors. First-time visitors see welcome messaging and overview content. Returning visitors see "Welcome back" and are directed to updates, events, or deeper engagement options.

Complexity
Medium
Level 4

Behavioral Triggers

Show targeted messaging based on visitor behavior. After viewing 3+ program pages, show "Ready to enroll?" prompt. After viewing donation page without giving, show gentle follow-up. Exit-intent popups for newsletter signup.

Complexity
Medium
Level 5

CRM-Integrated Personalization

Connect the website to donor/student management systems. Recognize logged-in users and show personalized content: "Thanks for your recent donation, [Name]" or "Your next lesson is Tuesday." Requires technical integration.

Complexity
High

Starting Points for NCMS

Practical first steps that work within Squarespace and existing resources

1

Audience-Based Navigation

Add clear entry points for key visitor types. A simple "I'm a..." section on the homepage lets visitors self-select into the most relevant experience.

Example: "I'm looking for lessons for my child" | "I want to support music education" | "I'm a current student/family"
2

Contextual Calls-to-Action

Match CTAs to page content. Program pages should lead to enrollment. Story pages should lead to giving. Event pages should lead to registration. Audit current pages for mismatched asks.

Example: Scholarship story page > "Help More Students Like Maria" CTA > Donation form
3

Progression Pathways

Design explicit "next steps" for each engagement level. Every page should answer: "What's the natural next action for someone at this stage?"

Example: Newsletter subscriber (Follower) > Invite to recital (Endorser) > Ask for donation after attending (Contributor)

The Opportunity

Most nonprofit websites treat all visitors the same. By recognizing where supporters are in their journey and meeting them with relevant content and asks, NCMS can improve both the visitor experience and engagement outcomes.

Start simple with audience-based navigation and contextual CTAs. These foundational changes require no new technology—just thoughtful organization of content you already have.
